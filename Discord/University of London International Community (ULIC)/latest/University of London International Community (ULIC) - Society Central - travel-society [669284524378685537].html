Mimi 🇦🇷 02-Nov-20 09:05 PM
Nowadays, Argentina is a country with hundreds of years of history. As many others, it is full of controversy, but it‚Äôs also very rich in culture. It is the southmost country in America, even having territory in Antartica. It is a Federal Republic with all its government officials (the President, members of Congress, Governors) elected through direct vote. It is made up of 23 provinces and the country‚Äôs capital city, the Autonomous City of Buenos Aires. Each province and the city have their own governments and constitutions but are subordinated to the national ones.
But, of course, the country didn‚Äôt just emerge fully formed. In the late 15th century, European explorers arrived in America. What we now know as Argentina became a Spanish colony, a part of what was known as el Virreynato del Río de la Plata (Viceroyalty of the River Plate). It remained that way until the 19th century when a wave of independence movements was sparked in Latin America. The 25th of May, 1810 the Revolution started. This gave rise to a series of armed confrontations with the Spanish, which culminated in the Argentinian Independence on the 9th of July, 1816. Unfortunately, the confrontations didn‚Äôt end then but transformed into a Civil War as two distinctive groups emerged with very different economic and representative policies: the Federales (who wanted each province to have their own government and earnings) and the Unitarios (who believed in a Central government). The flag, light-blue and white with a golden sun in the centre was sown by the women who had also sown the uniforms for the wars and was hoisted for the first time in 1812. There are many versions as to what the colours of the flag mean. The one which I like the most is that they stand for the sky and the freedom it represents. A predecessor of the flag is the rosette, in the same colours, which was already in use that 25th of May and which we still use during national holidays. Even during the Civil War, the country continued to advance towards unity, sanctioning the first Constitution in 1819. It was later reformed in 1826, when the name Argentina was officially adopted for the country. And then again in 1853 after the province of Buenos Aires separated from the rest of the country and became an independent unit, until losing the battle of Cepeda in 1859.
Since the end of the 19th century, geographically the country hasn‚Äôt changed much. As I told you some weeks ago, the waves of immigration did change the cultural composition of the country. Politically, the country has seen almost everything, including Military Dictatorships in the late 20th century, but luckily it has always seen ‚Äúla vuelta a la democracia‚Äù, the way back to democracy. Something I‚Äôve noticed is that schools here don‚Äôt really focus as much in the country‚Äôs history so many people barely know about it. Is this also true in your countries? I have been born and bred in Islamabad‚Äî the capital of Pakistan The city is one of the earliest known sites of human settlement in Asia. Some of the earliest Stone Age artifacts in the world have been found on the plateau, dating from 1 million to 500,000 years ago. When Pakistan gained independence in 1947, Karachi was its first capital. In 1960, Islamabad was constructed as a forward capital. It is considered by some as one of the most extensively and successfully planned cities in South Asia. (edited)
1
The most iconic site is the Faisal Mosque. Upon completion it was the largest mosque in the world and is currently the fifth largest mosque in the world and the largest in South Asia. It is located on the foothills of Margalla Hills in Islamabad. The mosque features a contemporary design consisting of eight sides of concrete shell and is inspired by a Bedouin tent. (edited)
Islamabad holds some of Pakistan's most prestigious museums such as Lok Virsa Museum, Institute of Folk and Traditional Heritage Shakarparian Park and prominent galleries such as the National Art Gallery and Gallery 6.
As Islamabad is situated on the Potohar Plateau, the remains of civilization descending from stone-age era include the Acheulian and the Soanian traditions and these are tourist landmarks. Islamabad has an array of historic landmarks that reflect the Hindu civilization that dates back to the 16th Century with examples such as Saidpur. Saidpur that is situated in Islamabad has progressed from a village to a sacred place that includes temples where the Hindu Mughal Commanders worshipped.
Islamabad boasts the highest literacy rate in Pakistan at 98% and has some of the most advanced educational institutes in the country. A large number of public and private sector educational institutes are present here. The higher education institutes in the capital are either federally chartered or administered by private organisations and almost all of them are recognised by the Higher Education Commission of Pakistan. High schools and colleges are either affiliated with the Federal Board of Intermediate and Secondary Education or with the UK universities education boards, O/A Levels, or IGCSE.
Most of the top ranked universities; National University of Sciences and Technology, COMSATS Institute of Information Technology and Pakistan Institute of Engineering & Applied Sciences, also have their headquarters in the capital. The world's second largest general university by enrolment, Allama Iqbal Open University is located in Islamabad for distance education.
This is the National University of Sciences and Technology (NUST). It is one of the most prestigious universities in the country.

Hey, Oscar from Peru here to show how we spend the holidays: 1. For many Peruvians, there is no Christmas without panetón (this is the local Spanish pronunciation of panettone, a northern Italian sweet bread studded with dried and candied fruit). Every Christmas you can see tens of brands, both industrial and artisanal, in every store and market in the country. Having a mug of hot chocolate or a café con leche, along with a big slice of panetón for breakfast, or a mid afternoon snack, is very common among Peruvians at this time of the year (a lot of people love it smothered with butter and strawberry jam ). Its popularity is so overwhelming that some brands sell the bread also in July, for the Independence celebrations, or even throughout the year. (edited)

2. The lines for picking up ur turkey . It is common that during the holidays the companies buy corporate packages of tickets for the employees. This tickets are exchanged for turkeys and sometimes piglets or chicken depending on the conditions of the purchase. There are a few big suppliers like San Fernando that do a heavy logistics operation in order to setup pickup points all around the country so that the people can exchange their tickets for a turkey. So around the week of the 25th you could see lines of thousands of people waiting on malls, supermarkets or special stores. Later either people cook them in their own houses or they will bring them to local bakeries in their neighborhoods to get them cooked. During the current circumstances they have set up an option for delivery however it seems that most prefer to wait in line (with masks and not so distant social distance ) to save a few bucks.
