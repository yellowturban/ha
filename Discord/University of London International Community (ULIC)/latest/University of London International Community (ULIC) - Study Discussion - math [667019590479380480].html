Hey, how did the ln become negative?
Alek 30-May-20 12:10 AM
Via integration by substitution @FN ; you want to find a function such that its derivative is 1 / (1-u). Now, the derivative of ln(1 - u) is [1 / (1 - u)] * [d/du 1-u] = - 1 / (1 - u), hence you need the extra minus to negate it once again, so that its positive like the derivative we want to obtain. More generally, you can (not always) do this when you have a composition of 2 functions in your integrand by expressing du as dg, where g(u) is a function of u (in this case g(u) = 1 / (1 - u)) and solving the integral in terms of g (then substituting back into the solution you obtain). Here is an example from mt1174 book.(edited)

Hey, does anyone know how to find the the partial derivative of the following function with respect to x
Can you use both quotient and product rule?
Alek 01-Jun-20 08:11 PM
You just have to use the power rule; remember that with partial derivatives, all of the other variables are treated as constants. In this case, ye^(2y) is a constant which can be moved in front of the derivative. Then, you want to differentiate 1 / x^a, which is the same as x^(-a). By the power rule, we have d/dx x^(-a) = (-a) * x^(-a - 1). The entire partial derivative then is df/dx = ye^(2y) * (-a) * x^(-a - 1). @FN(edited)

Here is a solution to an exercise you posted @FN; whenever you see something raised to the power of x and you want to differentiate it, you probably want to convert it to e^ln with that something in the logarithm, since then you can bring that x in the exponent down in front of the logarithm (by logarithm rules) and differentiate it using the chain rule. In calculating the 2nd-order derivative I use the already calculated 1st-order derivative.
1
Regarding your question about systems of equations - when solving a system, you need to find values of x, y that satisfy both equations simultaneously. If you have a convenient form such as the one in your example, you can extract the roots from both equations separately and see which pairs satisfy both. In this case, you can see that (x^2-x+1) in df/dy is always >0, so the only way for the 2nd equation to be 0 is for (3y^2-12) be 0, which is when y=+-2.
